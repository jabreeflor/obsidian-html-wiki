The Calculus of Boredom

A short defense of the empty hour, and the strange productivity that lives inside of it.

09 May 2026 · 7 min read #attention #essays

Boredom has acquired, in our era, the reputation of a small civic failure. To be bored at a dinner party is rude; to be bored at work is suspicious; to be bored on a Sunday afternoon, alone, with a book one has read before — this is, we are told, the entry-level form of a wasted life.

I want to argue the opposite. Boredom is not the absence of thought. It is the slack capacity that thought needs in order to occur — the same way a muscle requires rest, or a field a fallow year. The condition we have learned to treat as a deficiency is, more honestly, the precondition for any work that does not consist of mere reaction.

Marginal note The cognitive scientist Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi distinguished between flow and apathy; he was less interested in the territory between them, where most thinking actually happens.

What attention is for

Consider the paradox: every productive person you can name guards their time savagely, and yet the most useful hours in their week are the ones in which they did, by their own account, "nothing." See: Notes against productivity.

The mathematics here are inverted. The output of an hour of focused work is roughly linear in the number of such hours. The output of an hour of permitted boredom — call it the integration of small, unprompted thoughts — compounds. This is why so much of what passes for genius is really just somebody who had Tuesday afternoons free for thirty years.
