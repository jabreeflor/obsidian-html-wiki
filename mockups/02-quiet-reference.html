The Calculus of Boredom

A short defense of the empty hour, and the strange productivity that lives inside of it.

#attention #essays #reading Updated 09 May 2026 · 7 min

Boredom has acquired, in our era, the reputation of a small civic failure. To be bored at a dinner party is rude; to be bored at work is suspicious; to be bored on a Sunday afternoon, alone, with a book one has read before — this is, we are told, the entry-level form of a wasted life.

I want to argue the opposite. Boredom is not the absence of thought. It is the slack capacity that thought needs in order to occur — the same way a muscle requires rest, or a field a fallow year.

i
Note The cognitive scientist Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi distinguished between flow and apathy; he was less interested in the territory between them, where most thinking actually happens.

What attention is for #

Consider the paradox: every productive person you can name guards their time savagely, and yet the most useful hours in their week are the ones in which they did, by their own account, "nothing." See: Notes against productivity.

The mathematics here are inverted. The output of an hour of focused work is roughly linear in the number of such hours. The output of an hour of permitted boredom — call it the integration of small, unprompted thoughts — compounds.

A small experiment #

For one week, schedule one fallow afternoon. No phone, no agenda, no project. Note what surfaces. (See A field guide to attention for the protocol.)
